SOKY started with a single question: what if one of the oldest acts of care — immersing the body in water — could move with the person instead of confining them to a bowl? The answer took eight years to build. Simple inputs — water, salts, botanicals, warmth, time — better organized. No bowls. No stillness requirement. No compromise.

The inversion frustal cuff converts internal fluid pressure into sealing force. The more pressure builds, the tighter the seal becomes. No clamps. No adhesives. No power. Entirely passive and pressure-responsive.

The same garment base accepts interchangeable port modules — transforming a warm-water soak into an electrotherapy session, a botanical infusion, or a peptide delivery system. One ritual. Multiple modes. The platform scales from personal wellness to clinical care.

The SOKY Ritual — Five Steps. Always This Order.
01 — Don
Fold the cuff inward — into itself. This is the critical and non-intuitive first step. Pinch the fold, guide each finger fully into its sheath until the cuff rests flush at the wrist. No kinks, no gaps.
02 — Fill
Grip the folded cuff to hold the seal. Bring the opening under the tap. Fill to preferred level and temperature. Add salts, botanicals, or emollients — they go in with the water.
03 — Seal
Ball your fist. If the glove swells slightly — you're sealed. The cuff responds to pressure by holding more firmly, not less. Move freely or lay back.
04 — Soak
15–20 minutes of localized immersion. Walk. Read. Rest. The environment holds as you move. The ritual does not ask you to stop.
05 — Drain
Over a sink, slide off and pour out. Rinse inside with mild soap if needed, air dry fully, and store unfolded. A graceful exit invites return.
Critical — Donning

The cuff must always be folded inward — into itself to form the watertight/airtight seal. Never outward. This is where the product is used correctly or not at all.

Heat
Warm water 92°F to 104°F (33°C–40°C) boosts blood flow, relaxes muscle tissue, and supports joint mobility. SOKY's sealed environment retains heat for the full 15–20 min session without reheating. Ideal for morning stiffness and arthritis routines. Post-work hand fatigue and morning stiffness respond especially well to sustained thermal contact.
Cool
Cold immersion 50°F to 59°F (10°C to 15°C) reduces inflammation and numbs pain. Contrast bathing — alternating warm and cool — is clinician-recommended and SOKY makes it practical at home for the first time. For athletes and RSI recovery. No melting ice diluting the effect — the insulated seal maintains temperature throughout.
Electro
Model E transforms SOKY into a wearable electrotherapy platform. Iontophoresis delivers 80%+ sweat reduction for hyperhidrosis. TENS and EMS modes use the same sealed liquid-electrode environment. Covered by US Patent through 2043. The universal port accepts the electrode cap without modifying the garment.
Botanicals
Chamomile, calendula, lavender, green tea — botanical infusions at higher concentration than a basin. One tea bag in a SOKY glove delivers more active contact per cm² than the same bag in a two-liter bowl. Packs coming soon. Gentle enough for daily use.
Salts
Epsom salt, Dead Sea salt, Himalayan pink — SOKY's low volume means a tablespoon achieves what a full cup does in a basin. Supports muscle recovery, skin softening, and general relaxation. The sealed environment prevents evaporation and maintains mineral contact for the full session.
Actives

Clinical-grade soak packs in development for pharmacy-level precision at home. Because SOKY uses less water, your actives work harder at higher concentration right where they're needed.

The Story Behind the Seal
The founder lives with palmar hyperhidrosis — excessive sweating of the hands. The pivotal moment was having to refuse a child's hand. That experience sparked eight years of development, through a pandemic and personal upheaval, arriving at SOKY: a product and a philosophy built from real constraint, by someone who needed it. Built by hand in Workshop G — a garage in Riverside, California — and now developed across five countries by a team that believes the oldest forms of care deserve better delivery.
