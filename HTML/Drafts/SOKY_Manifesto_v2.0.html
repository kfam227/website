S O K Y

by NM Thera

Liquid Immersion Ritual System

For any care routine requiring localized liquid immersion, occlusion, or wearable therapeutic exposure.

SOKY started with a single, honest question: what if one of the oldest acts of care — immersing the body in water — could move with the person instead of confining them to a bowl, a tub, or a clinic station?

The answer took eight years to build. It started with a moment — a refusal to hold a child's hand because the skin condition made it impossible. That refusal became a conviction: the care had to exist. Not for some clinical abstraction of a patient, but for the person who already knows what they need and just needs the right tool to begin.

Soaking is ancient. Balneotherapy, hydrotherapy, contrast bathing, iontophoresis — practiced, studied, and trusted across cultures and centuries. What they share is access to a basin and the patience to stay still. SOKY removes the basin. It removes the stillness requirement. Not a clever glove. Not a lifestyle product. A transferable care architecture — wearable, modular, repeatable, and built to be shared.

Founding premise

SOKY is not just a glove or a sock. It is a platform for turning simple, ancient inputs — water, salts, botanicals, warmth, time — into localized rituals people can actually keep. The tools were always there. We removed the reason not to begin.

Spa-grade immersion. Anywhere.

I — What SOKY is

From one product to a whole grammar of care.

Define the platform before the platform gets defined too narrowly.

SOKY is a wearable soak system and an emerging care platform designed to localize useful routines around hands, feet, joints, and other body zones where immersion, warmth or coolness, hydration, and purposeful repetition can matter.

It belongs between the spa and the clinic, between self-care and serious routine, between discretion and efficacy. It takes something familiar — water plus time — and gives it structure: a contained environment, a simple workflow, and a form that can be repeated at home, supported in practice, carried while traveling, or adapted to context.

NM Thera builds the platform. The mission is to restore confidence, dignity, and daily rhythm by making localized immersion care accessible, repeatable, and evidence-aware — scaling from a first glove to a family of interoperable garments, and from a single protocol to a living commons of shared care knowledge that outlasts any product launch.

II — The ritual

Fill. Seal. Soak. Drain.

A product becomes a platform when the ritual is simple enough to be remembered.

The power of SOKY is not complexity. It is a clean four-step sequence that can carry many different kinds of care without asking the user to learn a new language every time.

SOKY has four steps — the same whether a person is soaking arthritic hands before bed, a clinician is guiding a patient through a post-procedure protocol, or an athlete is resetting after competition. The sequence is the shared language across every expression of the platform.

The SOKY Ritual · Four Steps · Same Every Time

1 — Fill
Pour water — warm or cool, depending on the use case — directly into the glove. Then add whatever the moment calls for: mineral salts, botanicals, emollients, a targeted solution. The enhancer goes in with the water, not after. What you put in is the beginning of the protocol.

2 — Seal
Fold the cuff inward. The self-sealing design contains the environment around the hand — portable, discrete, pressure-responsive. The ritual is no longer tied to a basin. It travels with the person. Move freely. The seal holds.

3 — Soak
Let time do its work. A standard session runs 15–20 minutes — enough for genuine immersion without demanding a change in schedule. Walk around. Rest. Read. The soak does not require stillness. That is the whole point.

4 — Drain
Finish cleanly. Remove the glove, drain the contents, rinse if needed, and air dry before storing. A graceful exit invites return. The ritual is designed to end without friction — so the next session is easier to begin than the last one was to finish.

The soak doesn't ask you to stop.

A single ritual can support many expressions.

The underlying workflow stays familiar even as the platform expands: home routines, clinician-adjacent support, performance recovery, seasonal care, body-part specific formats. The interface should feel calm on first use, obvious on second use, and quietly indispensable by the fifth.

VI — Science & safety

Clinically literate. Humanly honest.

Science is the basis for restraint as much as for confidence.

Immersion is not a decoration — it is a mechanism. The design has to know this. Occlusion increases stratum corneum hydration. Temperature modulates transdermal flux. Active modes introduce proportionally greater safety obligations.

NM Thera is clinically literate. Hands and feet are accessible, repeatable targets whose glabrous skin — palmar and plantar surfaces — has particular transport behavior: thick stratum corneum, high eccrine density, different transappendageal routes. Immersion maintains a high, uniform exposure environment for sustained time. The design controls that exposure carefully.

Occlusion & hydration

Containment is an active variable.

Prolonged immersion can expand stratum corneum hydration substantially — raising permeability but also increasing risk of maceration and irritation. Session length, seal design, and post-protocol recovery steps are designed around these effects, not incidental to them.

Thermal governance

Heat is a pharmacokinetic dial.

All heat-enabled paths include active temperature sensing and hard cutoffs. Warmth is a feature; uncontrolled heat is a hazard. The platform treats these as non-negotiable engineering requirements, not policy preferences.

Electro / iontophoresis

Built on demonstrated precedent.

Tap water iontophoresis for hyperhidrosis, with the glove patent (US 11,602,631) as a direct antecedent. Current limits, time limits, and sealed electrode modules are non-negotiable safety infrastructure. The evidence dossier precedes the claim.

Gas & pro modes

Promise paired with strict architecture.

CO₂ topical delivery signals microvascular response and tissue regeneration. Strong promise; strict design — gas-tight materials, single-patient architecture, clinical controls, and regulatory hygiene at every step. No overpromising.

Regulatory posture

Claims tied to evidence.

Consumer OTC language stays within safe, non-medical bounds. Pro and clinical modes carry explicit compliance strings, pre-specified thresholds, and evidence dossiers. NM Thera does not overclaim. It does not underbuild. Science is the basis for restraint as much as for confidence.

Infection control

Single-patient where it matters.

For clinical, wound-facing, or pelvic applications: single-patient architecture, disposable liners, validated disinfection, and documented infection-control SOPs are required. The platform distinguishes consumer paths from clinical paths structurally, not just in labeling.

VIII — The charter

Principles that can survive pressure.

This is the company logic underneath the aesthetic.

A strong manifesto should not just sound beautiful. It should constrain decisions when the easier option appears — when something cheaper, louder, more disposable, or less honest presents itself.

01

Localized care over generalized friction

Bring the routine to the body zone that needs it instead of asking people to improvise around the whole room.

02

Repair is respect

Reusability, easy cleaning, replacement parts, and design clarity are not extras. They are how trust is kept — through longevity, not novelty.

03

Calm is a feature

The product should feel steady, discreet, and emotionally light to use. Frantic products do not become rituals. Anxiety is not a user experience.

04

Science with humility

Be clinically literate, careful with claims, and open to evidence — without stripping the human meaning out of care. Informed practice, not performance of rigor.

05

Access over novelty

The best idea is the one people can actually use, understand, afford, repeat, and share with others. Innovation that cannot be accessed is decoration.

06

Community is infrastructure

Protocols, contributions, and trust networks make the platform stronger than any single launch moment. The people who extend SOKY are not users — they are stewards.

07

Safety is non-negotiable

Every mode carries its safety architecture before it ships. This is not a regulatory posture — it is a product conviction. The body deserves precision, not goodwill alone.

08

Knowledge should compound

A single product can help. A shared practice can last. The commons, the protocols, and the community library are how SOKY outlasts the launch.

09

Maximize what is already here

Water, time, warmth, and attention are not scarce. They are underused. SOKY's job is not to introduce new resources — it is to give the ones already available a better vessel. Conservation and care are the same instinct.

Closing statement

S O K Y

SOKY is not just a glove. Not just a sock. Not just another self-care object asking for attention. It is a way of organizing localized care so that simple materials — water, time, warmth, cooling, salts, botanicals, and thoughtful design — can become repeatable infrastructure for everyday life.

The tools were always there. Water has been the medium of care for longer than any modern product. What was missing was a vessel that moved with the person — that didn't demand a room, a basin, a dedicated hour carved out of an impossible schedule. SOKY is the vessel. The care was already yours.

The IP anchors us technically. The science and the charter guide our restraint. The commons and the steward-ownership structure ensure that what we build belongs to more than us. If executed to these commitments, SOKY will shift immersion therapy from an occasional, stationary ritual to daily, distributed infrastructure for human care — accessible to anyone willing to begin.

If we do this well, people will not only remember the product. They will remember the feeling that care became easier to begin, easier to repeat, and easier to share — because someone decided the resources already available to us deserved a better tool.

Spa-grade immersion. Anywhere.
